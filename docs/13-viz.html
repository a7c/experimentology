Mapping out a cholera epidemic (1854).

Voronoi plot showing boundary of neighborhoods to nearest pump.

In 1854, a deadly outbreak of cholera was sweeping through London. The scientific consensus at the time was that diseases like cholera spread through breathing poisonous and foul-smelling vapors, an idea known as the ‚Äúmiasma theory‚Äù (Halliday 2001Halliday, Stephen. 2001. ‚ÄúDeath and Miasma in Victorian London: An Obstinate Belief.‚Äù British Medical Journal 323 (7327): 1469‚Äì71.). An obstetrician and anesthesiologist named John Snow, however, had proposed an alternative theory: rather than spreading through foul air, he suggested that it was spreading a polluted water supply (Snow 1855Snow, John. 1855. On the Mode of Communication of Cholera. John Churchill.). To make a case for this idea, he started counting cholera deaths. He marked each case on a map of the area, and indicated the locations of the water pumps for reference. Furthermore, a can be drawn representing the region that was closest to each water pump, a technique which is now known as a Voronoi diagram. The resulting illustration makes a persuasive argument that cases clustered around an area called Golden Square, which received water from the same pump on Broad Street. Indeed, the outbreak died down shortly after the pump‚Äôs handle was removed. Although the precise causal role of these maps in Snow‚Äôs own thinking is still disputed, and it is likely that they were produced well after the incident (Brody et al. 2000Brody, Howard, Michael Russell Rip, Peter Vinten-Johansen, Nigel Paneth, and Stephen Rachman. 2000. ‚ÄúMap-Making and Myth-Making in Broad Street: The London Cholera Epidemic, 1854.‚Äù The Lancet 356 (9223): 64‚Äì68.), they nonetheless played a significant role in the history of visual explanations (Edward R. These waste sites were breeding grounds for mosquitos, which were the real culprits. Coincidentally, Seaman is also known as the first to introduce vaccines to the United States. What makes visualizations so useful, and what role should they play in experimental psychology? Simply put, data visualization is the act of ‚Äúmaking the invisible visible.‚Äù Our visual systems are remarkably powerful pattern detectors, and relationships that aren‚Äôt at all clear when scanning through rows of raw data can immediately jump out at us when presented in an appropriate graphical form (Zacks and Franconeri 2020Zacks, Jeffrey M, and Steven L Franconeri. 2020. ‚ÄúDesigning Graphs for Decision-Makers.‚Äù Policy Insights from the Behavioral and Brain Sciences 7 (1): 52‚Äì63.). Good visualizations delibrately harness this power and put it to work at every stage of the research process, from the quick sanity checks we run when first reading in our data to the publication-quality figures we design when we are ready to communicate our findings. Yet those powerful pattern detectors can also be a liability; if we‚Äôre not careful, we can easily be fooled into seeing patterns that are unreliable or even misleading.
